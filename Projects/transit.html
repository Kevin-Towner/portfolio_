NJ Transit Mobile App Functionality Overhaul

Introduction

It is widely known that commuting via public transportation is one of the least stressful ways of travel. Despite having control over more variables when using a personal vehicle, driving may be the most stressful form of travel. Using public transportation is also thought of as being more environmentally sustainable and better for the environment.

If using public transportation is less stressful, why are so many transit app UIs not reflective of this stress-free commutting process? When using the NJ Transit App I am able to buy tickets, view schedules and plan a trip. However, these are all separate processes and do not allow the user to streamline a true "Trip Planning" process.

Research, Qualitative Analysis & Personas

Conduct Interviews

I chose to interview four participants for my qualitative research. The participants comprise of 2 separate archetypes (Millennial & Boomer) and were chosen to illustrate a wide group of NJ-Transit users. The questions asked were designed to guage the way the NJ-Transit users view commuting to and from New York City via NJ-Transit ( RAIL ). Also, the questions were designed to gain opinions on GPS/Navigation applications and how they assist the user in their travels. As the role of the researcher, I wanted to know how the users feel when using public transportation, the stressors they encounter and other UIs the they may use to assist them where the current NJ-Transit App cannot.

Key Findings

The Millennial archetypes don't encounter as much difficulty when using public transportation. If they do happen to get lost, they don't seem to mind because they rely on their smart devices and/or GoogleMaps for assistance. Some features of GPS-based applications the Millennials enjoy are historic traffic patterns, traffic alerts and user feedback.
The Baby-Boomer archetypes are more likely to use their smart-devices for assistance via Uber. It's important to note that many Baby-Boomers aren't commuting to NYC for work as many are retired or simply go to the city for events and shows. With this in mind, taking an Uber is fairly straight-forward and leaves no desire to take the NYC subway.

Low-Fidelity Wireframes

The samples of the low-fidelity wireframes and information architecture show the concept of turning the NJ-Transit Application into a GPS-first application. With the landing page showing the user's current location within a map, the user will get a sense of reliability from knowing he or she will always know where he or she is.

Solution: "Plan Trip"

Upon thinking about the possible pros and cons of redesigning the app a GPS-forward service, I decided to shift my original concept. Instead, I provide a standard home-page with the 3 most standard services the app provides. Of these 3 services, the "Plan Trip" button will provide the user with everything the other 2 services does, and more.

Below will highlight the experience the typical user will go through with the changes I have made to NJ Transit's mobile app.

Finally...

Katrina is able to activate her ticket once she has made the purchase. Upon arriviving at her destination station, which is NY Penn Station, she will receive a notification with which will also be visible to her once she opens the app. From there, she will press the "Plan Trip" button and will be given the direction to her final destination via GPS navigation / Google-maps

Note: if she decides to exit out of the GPS map view, she will be able to access her current trip and pick up where she left off by pressing "Plan Trip". She will have an option to continue on her trip or start a new trip.
