Introduction

Upon signing up for a UX Design course at Brainstation in New York City, I wanted to gain a good understanding of my commute to the campus. I was not confident in my ability to navigate the city and/or city subway system without getting lost and I know others feel the same way I do.

I had already used the Mobile NJ Transit App in the past to purchase tickets, but I had never used the Trip Planning functionality because it doesn't have a lot to offer.

NJ Transit's current user interface

  • Identifying Objectives

    1. - Streamline the "Trip Planning" process from setting stations to assisting the users with commuting to their final destination
    2. - Create a more modern interface which passively pushes users towards planning their trip

Original Ideas (pre-research)

Originally, I had thought about the idea of designing NJ Transit as a GPS-based application first. In many ways, this application would mirror something akin to Google Maps, or even Waze. Therefore, many of my research questions were heavily designed to obtain user insights on pre-existing applications which commuters use to get to and from NY / NJ.

original wireframes illustrating a GPS interface as the homescreen

Research

Interview Questions and two interviewee responses.

Qualitative Research was conducted via interviewing four select people who commute to and from New York City. The four people are comprised of two arcehtypes; Millennials & Baby Boomers

Analyzing the interviees' responses, I separated direct quotes and replies into three categories; motivations, behaviors and pain-points

Interviewees' responses color coded and categorized by "motivations", "behaviors" and "pain-points."
Brainstorm of design solutions to some of the common interviewee responses

After categorizing the interviewee responses, I came up with functionality upgrade possibilities as well as constraints.

Personas

The results of the research weren't unpredictable, but it provided good information to be taken into consideration when wireframing and planning NJ Transit's user experience.

Millennials...
are generally not intimidated by the use of technology, nor were they really intimidated by the complexities of navigation and city-travel. What was most important to them was to have an app that allows them to conveniently plan their trips while utilizing features that other GPS applications have. Traffic patterns, subway maps and subway navigation is extremely important to maintain a strict sense of time and personal finance.

Baby Boomers...
are more concerned with navigation and the use of technology. Many would opt to arrive in the city and pay for the convenience of an Uber. The ability to purchase tickets and plan their NJ Transit arrivals/departures are priority. The ability for them see a map-view of where they are could also be helpful in the incident that they do get lost. Knowing that they can have a map on them is a nice cushion that many may use as navigation around New York City can be intimidating.

User Personas based on the two archetypes

Iteration One - 03.2019

My first iteration of NJ Transit's redesign was scoped down to providing the user with a streamlined trip-planning process. The inputs the user would be provided with, on the home page, would be spelled out for them and displayed with three main call to actions. The user would go through the trip-planning phase, with inputs only becoming available based on whether the preceeding forms were filled. Certain forms could be left unfilled depending on specific user preferences (final destinations and ticket purchasing).
